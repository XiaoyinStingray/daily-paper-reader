Abstract 

Dropout and Random Gradient Masking (RaM) are two training techniques used to im-prove performance in deep learning. Both techniques inject randomness into the training dynamics, but in significantly different ways: dropout applies random masks to the activa-tions in the forward pass, whereas RaM leaves the forward pass unchanged and instead masks the gradients. In particular, the noise induced by RaM in the parameter updates is unbiased, so standard explanations for the effectiveness of dropout, such as the penalization effect or the prevention of co-adaptation between neurons, do not apply to RaM. In this work, we show that the difference between the two methods disappears for ResNets in the large depth and width asymptotics: in the complete feature learning regime, they both converge to the same large-scale limiting dynamics. This asymptotic equivalence holds for several variants of dropout and RaM, including layerwise dropout as used in stochastic-depth ResNets, albeit at slower quantitative rates. In fact, we also show that several of these variants collapse to the same limit asymptotically. 

## 1 Introduction 

Dropout is a deep learning technique, introduced in [Hin+12], which randomly deactivates sub-sets of units during training. It is easy to implement, largely architecture-agnostic, and has been observed to improve test performance in a broad range of models, from AlexNet [KSH12] to ResNets [He+16] and Transformers [Vas+17]. Several explanations have been proposed for its effectiveness, including the prevention of co-adaptation between units [Hin+12], approximate model averaging [BS13], penalization through the bias induced in the gradient [MAV18], and implicit regularization induced by the variance of the dropout noise [WKM20]. 

Dropout in large-scale models. In order to disentangle these explanations, [CMY25] pro-posed to decompose the effect of dropout into three components: the propagation noise induced in the forward and backward passes; the penalization coming from the bias induced by using the same masks in the forward and backward passes; and the random masking of the gradient update. For two-layer networks, they show that, depending on the joint scaling of the width, dropout rate, and learning rate (LR), some or all of these effects asymptotically disappear, leading to a rich phase diagram. In particular, in the standard large-width limit with fixed dropout rate and

What is the asymptotic behavior of dropout in large-scale ResNets? 

Contributions. In this paper, we show that, for ResNets, as for shallow neural networks, only the random gradient masking effect persists in the large-scale regime. Specifically, we consider ResNets with fixed dropout rate in the joint width-and-depth limit, and in the вҖңcompleteвҖқ feature learning regime [Dey+26], and show that the dropout and RaM training dynamics are asymptotically equivalent. Our framework covers a number of dropout variants, characterized by their masking patterns, and we further show that several of these variants collapse to the same dynamics. From a technical perspective, we cannot rely on the mean-field viewpoint used in [CMY25], since it does not apply to practical architectures beyond the two-layer case. Instead, our rigorous analysis is made possible by the stochastic-approximation viewpoint on ResNet training dynamics recently introduced in [Chi25]. This equivalence challenges the conventional understanding of dropoutвҖҷs mechanisms in deep architectures, showing in particular that both the propagation noise and the penalization effects vanish in the limit. It also suggests that RaM may serve as a useful proxy for dropout in sufficiently large residual architectures. In fact, small-scale ablation experiments in [CMY25] suggest that RaM alone performs comparably to dropout, while the recent work [Joo+26] shows the вҖңsurprising effectivenessвҖқ of RaM in large-scale settings. Going further, these results suggest revisiting the theoretical explanation behind dropout to take into account its large-scale behavior: namely, why does RaM help training? 

Organization. In Section 2, we introduce the ResNet model and the training dynamics with dropout or Random Gradient Masking (RaM). Section 3 presents the main asymptotic equiva-lence result (Theorem 3.1) and derives the equations of the limiting model. Section 4 provides empirical validation of our theory, confirming the asymptotic equivalence of dropout and RaM in deep ResNets. Finally, the proofs are deferred to the appendix. 

1.1 Other related work 

Dropout. Ever since its introduction [Hin+12], dropout has been part of standard training pipelines [Vas+17; Rad+18; Dev+19; Dos+20; Hu+22; Ouy+22; Ram+22; Tay+22]. Its em-pirical success sparked the introduction of many analogous techniques and variants, such as DropPath/StochasticDepth [Hua+16], DropConnect [Wan+13], Shake-Shake [Gas17], ZoneOut for RNNs [Kru+17], SwapOut for ResNets [SHF16], DropBlock for CNNs [GLL18], and Stochas-ticPooling [ZF13], among many others. Our theoretical framework, detailed in Section 2, allows us to cover a number of these variants. In modern large-scale settings, the use of dropout was advocated for in [Xue+23; Liu+23], but also criticized in [LBM25], yielding a contrasted sit-uation. On the theoretical side, many works have studied the dropout penalty, including its closed forms [Aro+21] and its connections with classical regularization terms such as ridge regu-larization or path norms [WWL13; Aro+21; HL15; Mou+18]. The variance induced by dropout has also been studied for its effects on convergence properties and generalization [MA20; ZX24; Zha+23; WKM20] but generally without distinguishing between the random masking and the propagation noise. 2Random Gradient Masking (RaM). The technique that we refer to as Random Masking 

(RaM) is closely related to randomized block-coordinate descent [Nes12; Cot+11; Wri15]. In the optimization literature, random coordinate descent was introduced to reduce the computational cost of each step. This motivation does not apply in deep networks, where the full forward and backward passes have to be computed anyway. Nonetheless, RaM has been observed to improve the performance of neural networks in both small [CMY25] and large-scale settings [Joo+26]. Since RaM uses unbiased updates that depend on all units, it is unlikely that the explanations commonly invoked for the effectiveness of dropout (such as preventing co-adaptation or penal-ization effects) apply to it. 

Large-scale limits of ResNets. Our analysis relies on recent progress in the theory of large-scale training dynamics. The type of limit model that we obtain is a Neural Mean ODE [Lu+20; Din+22], which lies at the crossroads of the Neural ODE framework [Wei17; Che+18; Lu+20] and the mean-field analysis of 2LPs [NS17; CB18; MMN18; SS20; RV22]. The analysis of such models is an active line of research [JЕ S19; Iso23; BPV22; Bon+23; BPV25; DD25; GS25]. The link between such models and practical architectures has long been unclear, until [Chi25] showed that this limit is relevant for standard вҖңshapeвҖқ regimes where, for instance, the widths of all layers are proportional and the depth diverges. The hyperparameter scalings that we consider, inherited from the Neural Mean ODE literature, are also consistent with the so-called Complete Parameterization [Dey+26], which maximizes вҖңlocalвҖқ feature updates and has been proposed as the appropriate generalization of the maximal update parameterization [YH21] to the large-depth setting. Since it is not needed to obtain our main result, we do not derive the large embedding-dimension limit of our dynamics, but we note that it would be possible by adapting [CCM26]. See also [BCP24] for a prior derivation of the limit where all dimensions diverge that uses nonrigorous techniques from physics.

## 5 Conclusion 

In this work, we have established the asymptotic equivalence of dropout and Random Gradient Masking (RaM) in the training dynamics of large-scale ResNets (Theorem 3.1). We have also shown that all dropout variants asymptotically behave identically (Theorem 3.2). Our results rely on recent progress on the mathematical analysis of large ResNets. This equivalence challenges the conventional understanding of dropoutвҖҷs mechanisms in deep architectures, and our work leaves open a number of questions: 

вҖў In practical large-scale settings, can we replace dropout by RaM altogether? In fact, recent works [Joo+26; LBM25] imply that RaM compares favorably to dropout, suggesting that the finite-size effects of propagation noise and penalization of dropout could be undesirable. 

вҖў Why does RaM improve the test performance? A partial explanation was proposed in [CMY25], which showed that for wide two-layer neural networks, RaM makes it possible to increase the effective learning rate of active units while remaining in the stable region of GD. This property, which relies on the fact that the output of the model is an average of the con-tributions of the units, should also hold in the ResNet setting. However, this is far from 12 a full answer to the question. In particular, it would be interesting to show a statistical benefit of RaM. 13 References
