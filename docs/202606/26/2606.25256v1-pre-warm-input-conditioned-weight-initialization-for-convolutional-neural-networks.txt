# Pre-Warm: Input-Conditioned Weight Initialization for Convolutional Neural Networks

Abstract 

We introduce Pre-Warm, a simple yet effective zero-training-cost method for data-conditioned initial-ization of the first convolutional layer. Before the first forward pass, Pre-Warm extracts mean-centered local patches from a single training batch, clusters them with MiniBatchKMeans, applies inverse Man-hattan spatial weighting, and uses the resulting centroids to initialize half of the first-layer filters (the remainder retain Kaiming initialization). We derive closed-form rules for all hyperparameters except a single insensitive scale parameter, though we derive a Kaiming parity bound on scale from patch dimensionality. For grayscale datasets we use Otsu’s foreground density; for natural color images we use the mean L2 norm of mean-centered patches. Both rules accurately predict the optimal patch count observed in grid search. Across five standard benchmarks - MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, CIFAR-10, SVHN, and CIFAR-100 -and 8-seed paired experiments, Pre-Warm yields statistically significant accuracy improvements over standard Kaiming initialization (p ¡ 0.05 on all datasets, p = 0.0007 on SVHN with 8/8 wins, p = 0.0033 on CIFAR-100 with 7/8 wins). The method adds negligible overhead, requires no architectural changes, and integrates into existing training pipelines with only a few lines of code. Pre-Warm demonstrates that even a lightweight, input-dependent signal can meaningfully improve optimization trajectories in modern convolutional networks. 

# 1 Introduction 

The choice of initial weights determines the starting geometry of the loss landscape. Kaiming initialization (He et al., 2015) and Glorot initialization (Glorot & Bengio, 2010) are statistically principled - they preserve activation variance across layers - but make no reference to the actual data the network will process. Every dataset, regardless of its local structure, receives an identical initialization drawn from a scaled random distribution. We propose that even a lightweight, zero-cost signal from the data can meaningfully inform the starting state of the first layer. This is not a claim about replacing the optimization process, but about giving it a better starting point in a principled and automatic way. Pre-Warm targets only the first convolutional layer, adds no trainable parameters, requires no gradient steps, and completes in under 0.1 seconds on a T4 GPU. The key insight is that k-means on mean-centered local patches naturally discovers the dominant edge and texture patterns in the image distribution - precisely what the first convolutional layer is known to learn (Goodfellow et al., 2016). Seeding those filters with data-derived centroids reduces the number of gradient steps required to encode this structure. 

# 2 Related Work 

Data-dependent initialization Kr¨ ahenb¨ uhl et al. (2016) initialize convolutional weights using statistics from mini-batch forward passes. LSUV (Mishkin & Matas, 2016) normalizes each layer’s output variance iteratively using real data. Both methods operate at the dataset level and require forward passes through the network. Pre-Warm is per-batch, requires no forward pass, and operates entirely in input space at negligible cost. 1

Unsupervised pre-training Greedy layer-wise pretraining (Hinton et al., 2006) uses RBMs or autoen-coders to provide informed initializations before supervised fine-tuning. These methods are computationally expensive and have been largely supplanted by modern normalization and residual architectures (He et al., 2016). Pre-Warm recovers the core intuition - use unsupervised structure to inform initialization at essentially zero cost. 

Dictionary learning and sparse coding Olshausen & Field (1996) showed that sparse coding on natural image patches produces Gabor-like filters matching V1 receptive fields. K-means on local patches is a tractable approximation of this process. Pre-Warm applies this observation directly to filter initialization, with a principled derivation of how many patches to sample. 

Meta-learning MAML (Finn et al., 2017) learns an initialization that adapts quickly to new tasks through second-order optimization. Pre-Warm can be interpreted as a static, zero-cost approximation of this principle: conditioning initial parameters on input statistics rather than learning the conditioning mechanism. It recovers a fraction of the benefit at orders-of-magnitude lower cost.

## 3.3 Training protocol 

Each run trains for N = 5000 optimization steps with Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015), batch size 256, and cross-entropy loss. The learning rate follows a cosine annealing schedule (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) from ηstart 

to ηend over the full 5000 steps. The grayscale datasets and CIFAR-10 use ηstart = 3 × 10 −3, ηend = 3 × 10 −5;SVHN uses a lower ηstart = 5 × 10 −4, ηend = 3 × 10 −6, which we found necessary for stable convergence on that dataset. Every configuration is run over 8 random seeds, {42 , 7, 13 , 99 , 2025 , 1, 8, 21 }, with the seed controlling weight initialization, data shuffling, and the k-means initialization so that the Pre-Warm and Kaiming runs at a given seed are exactly paired. All experiments run on a single NVIDIA T4 GPU. 

## 3.4 Pre-Warm configuration 

The conditioned runs apply Pre-Warm to conv1 before the first forward pass using the design rules derived in Section 4. Three parameters follow directly from architecture dimensions: nclusters = F/ 2 = 16 (half the filter bank is conditioned, half retains Kaiming initialization), patch size = K = 3 (patches must match the filter receptive field), and scale σ, which controls the amplitude of the conditioned filters. The fourth parameter, npatches , is derived from dataset statistics and differs by image type. 

Grayscale datasets For MNIST and Fashion-MNIST, pixel histograms are strongly bimodal - dark back-ground versus bright foreground strokes - making Otsu’s method (Otsu, 1979) a reliable foreground density estimator. We apply npatches = ⌊(F/ 4) · (1 /d )⌋, where d is the fraction of pixels above the Otsu threshold 

τ ∗. On MNIST, τ ∗ = 0 .4434 gives d = 0 .1373 and npatches = 58; on Fashion-MNIST, τ ∗ = 0 .3809 gives 

d = 0 .3602 and npatches = 22. The scale is σ = 0 .20 for both datasets. 

Color datasets For CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and SVHN, Otsu’s bimodal assumption breaks down: all three datasets have near-unimodal intensity distributions that pixel-level thresholds cannot distinguish. We instead derive npatches from the mean L2 norm of mean-centered patches - the same patches the conditioner already extracts - via npatches = ⌊(F/ 4) · c · ∥ ˜p∥2⌋ with c ≈ 6.65. On CIFAR-10 we formulated a prediction of 28. On SVHN we formulated a prediction of 16. For CIFAR-100 the estimated number of patches needed is 16. The scale is σ = 0 .25 for all three color datasets, compensating for the threefold increase in patch dimensionality (3 × 3 × 3 = 27 versus 9) and keeping conditioned filter energy near the Kaiming standard-deviation scale for Cin = 3.

# 4 Method 

## 4.1 Overview 

Pre-Warm conditions the first convolutional layer of a network on a single batch of training images before any gradient step is taken. The core pipeline is identical across all datasets: extract local patches, mean-center them, run MiniBatchKMeans, apply inverse Manhattan distance spatial weighting to the centroids, normalize to a target scale, and write the resulting tensors into the first nclusters filters of conv1 . The remaining filters retain Kaiming initialization, preserving the random diversity that allows the network to represent patterns not captured by k-means. The method has four hyperparameters: patch size , n clusters , n patches , and scale . The central empirical contribution of this section is that three of the four are governed by closed-form rules derived from architecture dimensions and dataset statistics, leaving scale as the only free parameter - and scale is empirically insensitive within a dataset-type-specific range. We derive these rules in two stages: first via a full grid search on Fashion-MNIST, which establishes the grayscale laws, then via extension to natural color images.

We established the design rules via a grid search on Fashion-MNIST, sweeping all four hyperparame-ters: patch size ∈ { 3, 5}, nclusters ∈ { 8, 16 , 32 , 64 }, npatches ∈ { 10 , 15 , 20 , 25 , 30 , 50 , 100 }, and scale ∈{0.05 , 0.1, 0.2, 0.3}, over 8 seeds each, evaluated by paired t-test on convergence loss at 500 steps. The results were decisive on three of the four axes. 

patch size = K All patch size = 3 configurations produced positive loss deltas; no patch size = 5 configuration achieved significance. When patch size > K , centroids must be center-cropped to match the filter shape, discarding the outer ring where edge-transition information is concentrated. The rule is: patch size must equal kernel size. 

nclusters = F/ 2 Only nclusters = 16 (= F/ 2) achieved significant results. Too few conditioned filters (nclusters ≪ F/ 2) provide insufficient signal coverage; too many ( nclusters ≥ F ) overwrite the entire filter bank, eliminating the Kaiming diversity that allows the network to represent patterns not captured by k-means. Conditioning exactly half the filter bank balances structured prior with stochastic exploration. 

npatches : Otsu foreground density rule Statistical significance appeared exclusively at npatches = 25 and disappeared at both npatches = 20 and npatches = 30, revealing a sharp empirical threshold. Below the threshold, informative patches are too sparse per cluster for reliable centroid estimation; above it, background patches - Fashion-MNIST images are approximately 65% background - dilute centroids toward near-zero vectors that carry no more information than random Kaiming weights. ## 4.4 Extension to Color Datasets 

Applying the grayscale rules directly to CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and SVHN required revisiting only the 

npatches formula and the scale. The patch size = K and nclusters = F/ 2 rules transfer unchanged: the 5geometric arguments that govern them are architecture-level properties independent of channel count or pixel distribution. 

Scale The grayscale scale of σ = 0 .20 required a small upward adjustment to σ = 0 .25 for color images. The reason is dimensional: a 3 × 3 × 3 color patch spans 27 dimensions versus the 9 of a grayscale patch. Global normalization distributes centroid energy over a larger space, so a slightly larger scale is needed to maintain the same effective signal-to-noise ratio relative to the random Kaiming weights of downstream layers. The optimal value σ = 0 .25 sits near the Kaiming standard deviation for Cin = 3 ( σKaiming =p2/fan in = p2/27 ≈ 0.272), suggesting that Kaiming parity is a useful upper bound for the scale. 

npatches for color images: patch L2 norm rule The Otsu-based formula in Equation 5 cannot be applied directly to natural color images because its bimodal foreground/background assumption breaks down. The mean L2 norm of mean-centered patches, ∥˜p∥2, captures exactly what the pixel distribution cannot: the average texture energy available to k-means after brightness is removed. Patches with larger norm carry richer edge and texture variation; k-means with a fixed codebook size k = F/ 2 needs proportionally more samples to populate a well-conditioned codebook over a high-energy distribution. ## 5.1 Final Accuracy 

Pre-Warm produces a statistically significant improvement in final test accuracy on all five datasets at the 

α = 0 .05 level (Table 3). The strongest result is on SVHN, where every one of the eight seeds improves (8 /8 wins), the mean gain is +0 .48 accuracy points, and p = 0 .0007. CIFAR-100 shows the second largest absolute gain: +0 .68 accuracy points (7 /8 wins, p = 0 .0033), notable given that the task has 100 classes and the baseline accuracy sits near 43%. The remaining datasets show smaller but consistent percentage gains: +0 .23 points on CIFAR-10, +0 .16 on Fashion-MNIST, and +0 .05 on MNIST. The absolute gains on the grayscale datasets are small because both initializations already operate near the task ceiling - MNIST accuracy exceeds 99 .2% under both conditions - yet the paired improvement remains significant. The effect is therefore not confined to the grayscale regime in which the method was originally developed: it transfers consistently across natural color images and scales to the harder fine-grained classification setting of CIFAR-100.

## 5.3 Limitations and Future Work 

While Pre-Warm demonstrates consistent gains across five standard benchmarks, several limitations remain. First, the method has only been evaluated on small-to-medium convolutional architectures and relatively simple datasets. Its effectiveness on larger models (e.g., ResNet, Vision Transformers) or more complex tasks (object detection, segmentation) is untested. Second, the current design conditions only the first convolutional layer; extending the approach to deeper layers or multiple layers simultaneously is a natural direction for future work. Third, although the hyperparameter rules generalize well, the scale parameter still requires modest dataset-type-specific tuning (0.20 for grayscale vs. 0.25 for color). A fully automatic, theoretically grounded formula for scale remains an open question. Finally, the computational overhead, while negligible on modern hardware, could become more noticeable when training on extremely large datasets or when the first batch is not representative. Despite these limitations, Pre-Warm provides a simple, practical, and theoretically motivated improve-ment to standard initialization practices with virtually zero cost. 

# 6 Conclusion 

We presented Pre-Warm, a lightweight, zero-training-cost method that conditions the initialization of the first convolutional layer on the statistics of a single training batch. By clustering mean-centered local patches and 8seeding half the filters with the resulting centroids, Pre-Warm injects meaningful data-dependent structure into the network while preserving the diversity of Kaiming initialization in the remaining filters. Through careful empirical analysis on Fashion-MNIST, we derived simple closed-form rules for all hy-perparameters except a single scale parameter. These rules successfully generalize to MNIST, CIFAR-10, SVHN, and CIFAR-100, yielding statistically significant accuracy improvements across all five benchmarks (p ¡ 0.05) with particularly strong results on SVHN (p = 0.0007, 8/8 wins) and CIFAR-100 (p = 0.0033, 7/8 wins, +0 .68 accuracy points on a 100-class task). Pre-Warm demonstrates that even a minimal, input-aware signal at initialization can meaningfully im-prove optimization trajectories in modern convolutional networks. Its negligible computational cost, ease of implementation, and compatibility with standard architectures (including BatchNorm) make it a practical drop-in enhancement for a wide range of computer vision pipelines. Future work includes extending the approach to deeper layers, exploring its interaction with larger ar-chitectures, and developing a fully unified theoretical framework for the scale parameter across diverse data regimes.
