Abstract 

Understanding the generalization performance of over-parameterized neural networks has become a central topic in deep learning theory. While recent advances, particularly works under the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) regime, have shed light on the behavior of shallow architectures, the statistical generalization properties of deep neural networks (DNNs), especially in regression tasks, remain far less understood. In this paper, we make significant progress toward closing this gap by providing a comprehensive generalization analysis of DNNs trained using gradient-based methods. First, we establish, for the first time, a crucial connection between the learning dynamics of a DNN with smooth activation functions trained via gradient-based methods and those of kernel methods, showing that gradient-based methods on over-parameterized DNNs can fully inherit the favorable learning dynamics of their kernel counterparts. Building on this connection and the well-established optimality of kernel methods, we derive the first known minimax-optimal rates for the excess population risk of both gradient descent (GD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD), under the assumption that network width scales polynomially with the sample size. Our results demonstrate that, with sufficient width, DNNs trained by GD or SGD can achieve generalization performance comparable to kernel-based methods. 

# 1 Introduction 

A central mystery in deep learning is how neural networks, despite being highly non-convex and heavily over-parameterized, are able to achieve near-zero training error while still generalizing well to unseen data. This paradox has sparked a surge of research aimed at understanding the convergence and generalization behavior of neural networks [1, 2, 6, 7, 15, 38, 41, 49]. The Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK), introduced by [ 20 ], has become one of a foundational tool for understanding the behavior of training dynamics for neural networks, especially those trained using gradient-based methods such as gradient descent (GD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The core idea here is to linearize the neural network around its random initialization, which enables the evolution of the network during training to be closely approximated by a kernel method associated with the corresponding NTK. This framework establishes a powerful connection between the evolution of a neural network during training process and the behavior of kernel methods in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) induced by the NTK, allowing insights from the kernel methods to inform our understanding of neural networks. Following this perspective, the influential work [ 34 ] showed that for regression problems, shallow neural networks trained by SGD can achieve generalization performance on par with their kernel counterparts. ## 1.1 Related Work 

There exists a substantial body of literature dedicated to understanding the generalization ability of neural networks. We briefly review the most relevant work to ours. 

NTK Approach. A rapidly growing line of work has analyzed over-parameterized neural networks in the NTK regime, yielding generalization bounds for models trained via gradient methods, with significant progress in classification settings. Under margin separation assumptions, [ 27 , 21 , 33 ] derived generalization error bounds for shallow networks with ReLU and smooth activations. [ 6 , 7] further provided algorithm-dependent misclassification bounds for deep ReLU networks trained by GD and SGD, assuming that the data are separable under certain random feature models [36]. In contrast, understanding of generalization for regression problems remains limited. The work [ 2 , 23 ] established generalization bounds for GD applied to shallow ReLU networks, under favorable NTK Gram matrix assumptions. Later, [34, 32] removed the this assumption and derived minimax-optimal excess risk rates for GD and one-pass SGD, respectively, under smooth activations. [ 5] improved these results in capacity-independent case by reducing the required network width. The most related work is [ 47 ], which analyzed the uniform convergence of the NTK for deep ReLU networks at Gaussian initialization. Building on this, they proved that the prediction error of one-pass SGD converges to zero in expectation, though explicit rates were not provided. Notably, sharp excess population risk bounds for DNNs remain an open challenge. 

Uniform Convergence Approach. Another prominent approach to understanding generalization ability of neural networks is based on uniform convergence. This framework leverages complexity measures such as Rademacher complexity and covering numbers to bound the capacity of the hypothesis class, resulting in algorithm-independent generalization guarantees [ 3, 16 , 17 , 30 , 35 ]. Using this framework, generalization bounds of order O(1 /√n) have been obtained by trading off sample fit against hypothesis complexity [ 2, 10 , 21 , 25 , 26 ]. [ 25 ] improved population risk bounds to O(1 /n ) via local Rademacher complexity. Following this line of work, recent studies [ 4 , 13 , 14 , 22 ]established generalization bounds for shallow and deep neural networks trained with GD. In particular, [ 22 ] derived a nearly optimal convergence rate of O(n− 2p 

> 2p+d+ϵ

) for any ϵ > 0, for a linear combination of Kn deep smooth networks, under the assumption that the target function is (p, C )-smooth. Here, Kn is a parameter that depends on both sample size n and dimension d.

Algorithmic Stability Approach. More recently, the algorithmic stability framework has emerged as a powerful tool for analyzing generalization in neural networks trained via gradient-based methods. For regression tasks, [ 38 , 24 , 44 ]demonstrated that shallow networks with smooth activations trained via GD or SGD can achieve excess risk bounds of 

O(1 /√n). These results rely on the observation that the empirical risk function is smooth and weakly convex, with convexity parameters decaying with network width. [ 40 ] advanced this line of work by establishing a self-bounded weak convexity property for logistic loss under realizable settings, while [ 41 ] extended these insights to deeper networks. Very recently, [ 45 , 46 ] provided population risk bounds for GD and SGD, respectively, for structured Kolmogorov-Arnold networks via on-averaged argument stability methods. 3Notation. Our results demonstrate that, as long as the network width scales polynomially with n and d, SGD with deep smooth networks can recover the classical results of SGD in the kernel setting (see [ 12 , 28 ]). This provides an affirmative answer to the aforementioned open question. 

Comparison with existing works. The most closely related works to ours are [ 5] and [ 34 ], which study the generalization performance of one-pass SGD for shallow neural networks with smooth activation functions. In particular, [ 34 ]established the minimax-optimal excess risk rate O(n− 2β 

> 2β+γ

) under Assumptions 1, 2 and 3, where β ∈ [1 /2, 1] .However, to achieve this optimal rate, the network width m is required to grow exponentially with the sample size n.The recent work [ 5 ] improved upon this result in the capacity-independent setting (i.e., γ = 1 ), reducing the required network width from exponential to polynomial in n. In contrast, we focus on deep network architectures and, for the first time, provide minimax-optimal excess risk rates for multi-pass SGD in this setting for any 2β + γ > 1. Moreover, our results also apply to the case where fρ /∈ H K (i.e., β < 1/2), which significantly extends the applicability of [ 34 ], even beyond shallow networks. 

Limitation. A potential limitation of the NTK approach lies in its reliance on linear approximations to evaluate network performance during training. In practical applications, local linearity may not always hold, particularly when the number of training iterations is large or the network width is insufficient. Our results depend only on a moderate number of iterations, under which the use of linear approximations is theoretically justified and feasible within our framework. However, in practice, training often involves a larger number of iterations. We therefore recognize the importance of exploring alternative approaches to study the generalization performance of neural networks and view this as an important direction for future research. 95 Conclusion 

In this paper, we establish a key connection between the learning dynamics of DNNs and those in the RKHS by leveraging over-parameterization. Building on this connection, we demonstrate that DNNs with smooth activations trained via GD/SGD can achieve the minimax-optimal excess risk rate O(n− 2β 

> 2β+γ

), provided the network width satisfies 

m ≳ Poly (n, d ). Our results show that GD/SGD with DNNs can attain generalization performance comparable to classical gradient methods in the kernel setting. Several avenues for future research remain. First, it would be valuable to reduce the exponential dependence on depth L in the generalization bounds and the associated width requirement m.Second, extending the analysis to other architectures, such as convolutional or residual networks, represents another promising direction.
