## Abstract 

Segmentation is a fundamental task in computer vision, underpinning pixel-level scene understanding and serving as a cornerstone for applications ranging from autonomous perception to medical image analysis. For complex referring seg-mentation, recent methods pair large vision-language models with segmentation decoders: the former analyzes the image and prompt, while the latter predicts the target mask. Although reinforcement learning improves reasoning-intensive vision-language systems, trainable tools such as segmentation decoders are typically optimized separately with differentiable objectives, and the principled integration of such objectives into reinforcement learning remains underexplored. Thus, we introduce group relative tool optimization (GRTO), a mathematically grounded framework for jointly optimizing a policy with differentiable tool use. GRTO reuses group relative policy optimization (GRPO) rollouts to optimize the auxiliary tool objective, letting decoder gradients complement policy rewards. Further, we derive Bootstrapped-GRTO (B-GRTO), a pretraining method that cheaply boot-straps the tool, leading to faster convergence and superior performance. Across three challenging referring segmentation settings, B-GRTO results in substantial improvements over plain GRPO, matching or surpassing domain-specific state-of-

## 1 Introduction 

Referring segmentation tasks require a model to produce a pixel-level mask for a target specified by natural language. Unlike conventional semantic segmentation, where each pixel is assigned to one of a fixed set of predefined categories, referring segmentation conditions the output on an open-ended prompt. Flexible target descriptions are central to open-ended visual interfaces, robotics, medical image analysis, safety-critical perception, and open-world perception, where segmentation should follow intent rather than fixed categories [ 6, 9 , 65 ]. However, in challenging cases, the prompt may initially be ambiguous or under-specified. Then, the model needs to determine which object is being referred to before it can segment. The task therefore combines dense mask prediction with prompt disambiguation. Existing methods tend to address these two problems separately. When the segmentation decoder is insufficient for a target domain, it is trained with supervised fine-tuning (SFT) to improve mask quality. This is effective when the object to segment is already well specified, but it does not solve cases where the prompt itself requires nontrivial interpretation. Interpreting difficult prompts also requires world knowledge and the ability to connect language in context to high-level visual semantics. This motivates systems that combine dense segmentation decoders with multimodal large-language models (MLLMs). Their representations are better suited to resolve the complex, nonlocal, and compositional reasoning needed to determine what should be segmented [ 12 , 15 , 25 ]. Reinforcement learning (RL) offers a natural way to optimize the reasoning policy because the desired intermediate interpretation is rarely annotated. Training data usually provides only the final mask, not the disentangled prompt representation. RL can improve the MLLM using final-mask rewards, letting it explore prompt alternative interpretations and reinforce those yielding better masks [ 31 , 32 , 66 ]. However, existing RL-based approaches typically update the MLLM while keeping the segmentation decoder fixed. This ignores differentiable tool feedback that could adapt the decoder to the same successful episodes guiding the policy. This reveals an important underexplored setting: referring segmentation where both sources of difficulty are present at the same time. In many realistic applications, the prompt is not fully explicit and the visual domain is also challenging. A remote-sensing user may ask for “heaven for sunbathers and swimmers” while a camouflage dataset may require identifying the “camouflaged object.” Such cases require both referring-expression reasoning and adaptation to subtle, domain-specific visual cues. Improving only reasoning leaves mask quality decoder-limited; improving only the decoder assumes the target is known. A method for this setting must therefore jointly optimize the reasoning policy and the segmentation tool, as outlined in Figure 1. More generally, this is an instance of trainable tool use: when a reasoning policy calls a tool, policy-level rewards need not be the only learning signal. However, this joint optimization is asymmetric: the policy is large and costly to improve through action sampling, whereas the tool is often lightweight and cheaply updated with differentiable objectives. This suggests that effective methods should exploit cheaper opportunities to pre-align and refine the tool before reinforcement learning. Crucially, such pre-alignment should not merely fit the tool to the behavior of the initial policy; it must anticipate and support the policy after training, so that the tool remains aligned with the improved reasoning strategy that reinforcement learning is expected to produce. We introduce bootstrapped group relative tool optimization (B-GRTO) , a framework for jointly optimizing a reasoning policy and trainable differentiable tool within group relative policy optimiza-tion (GRPO) [ 52 ], without limiting tool training to policy-optimization speed. B-GRTO has two stages: bootstrapped tool optimization (BTO) , which cheaply pre-aligns the tool to the theoretical 

post-trained policy, and group relative tool optimization (GRTO) , which integrates tool fine-tuning into policy RL. We evaluate B-GRTO on three challenging settings in the referring segmentation domain: remote sensing segmentation, camouflaged object detection, and reasoning segmentation, where it delivers substantial gains over conventional policy-only GRPO, competing with in-domain state-of-the-art (SoTA) architectures. 2Prompts

## 2 Related works 

Multimodal LLMs for Language-Instructed Segmentation. 

Early approaches to language-guided segmentation tightly couple a large language model with a pixel decoder via special segmentation token [ 25 , 62 , 23 , 58 , 47 , 60 , 55 ] into the LLM’s vocabulary whose embedding is decoded by SAM [ 24 ] into a binary mask. Several designs substitute the token with a full code-book [ 49 , 68 ] or a separate text encoder [ 36 ]. A common limitation across this entire family is that all components are optimized with standard cross-entropy on fixed annotation sets, which can overfit to the label distribution, limiting generalization, and does not allow the model to explore novel approaches that could lead to superior results. 

Decoder as a tool and Reinforcement Learning. 

The success of group relative policy optimization (GRPO) [ 52 ] for reasoning in language models has given rise to a second generation of segmentation approaches that use it to learn robust and accurate tool-use of SAM [48]. Earlier versions use it only as a test-time tool, training on bounding-box IoU rewards [ 5, 31 , 32 , 72 ]. Later works introduce tool-use at training to access end-to-end mask IoU rewards [ 21 , 66 ]. Several methods suggest tool-prompting in latent space, trained with a segmentation loss [ 73 , 37 ], and attempts are made to employ these methods in specialized domains, e.g. in remote sensing [ 63 , 67 ] or pathology [ 35 ]. Further, agentic setups with multi-turn tool-use are also investigated with promising results [ 2, 10 , 18 , 74 ]. These approaches demonstrate that RL-refined language actions reasoning measurably lift segmentation quality even when the segmentation tool itself is held fixed. However, most of the methods practically assume SAM to be a perfect tool and do not explore the potential gain in treating SAM as a trainable component. 

SAM Fine-Tuning for Specialized Domains. 

Although SAM exhibits strong zero-shot segmentation capabilities, its performance degrades on out-of-distribution imagery. Fine-tuning SAM on well-camouflaged animals, cast shadows, and medical images is shown to substantially recover accuracy in these failure modes [ 3 ]. Analogously, in remote sensing, fine-tuned SAM variants outperform prompt-tuned baselines on building footprints, solar panels, sinkholes, and dead-tree detection [ 1]. However, these studies assume perfect prompts, whereas in real-world scenarios prompts are often ambiguous or too long to be tokenized by SAM. Motivated by these limitations, we study referring segmentation as a trainable tool-use problem in which both the reasoning policy and the segmentation decoder may need to adapt. Rather than treating the decoder as a fixed reward component or fine-tuning it independently under perfectly specified prompts, we propose B-GRTO: a two-stage framework that first bootstraps the tool toward rollouts expected to be reinforced, and then jointly optimizes the policy and tool within GRPO (Figure 2).

B-GRTO in referring segmentation. In our setting, the policy is InternVL3.5-8B [ 57 ], which generates concise noun phrases and bounding boxes for the referred object instances. These concepts and boxes are parsed as prompts for SAM3 [ 2] in promptable concept segmentation mode, producing segmentation masks conditioned on the image and generated prompts. Finally, we apply deterministic spatial filtering by setting all pixels outside the generated boxes to background, so the boxes serve as both prompts and hard spatial constraints. 

## 4 Experiments 

We select InternVL3.5-8B as our VLM backbone across experiments due to its strong performance across various domains and tasks including visual grounding [ 57 ], and SAM3 as our mask genera-tor [ 2]. We optimize SAM3 using a differentiable BCE + soft-IoU surrogate, while evaluating the VLM with final-mask IoU as the reward signal. For the detailed experimental setup, see Appendix E. We conduct experiments on three challenging referring segmentation settings: 

Camouflage detection. Camouflaged objects commonly exhibit much lower contrast and higher appearance similarity to their surroundings compared to common segmentation targets. COD10K [ 13 ]and CAMO [ 26 ] are widely used camouflaged object segmentation benchmarks with pixel-level object mask annotations. As these datasets do not provide a separate validation split, a recent line of work has started using the testing set as validation set [ 3, 70 , 61 ]. We instead follow the protocol of splitting the training set into train and validation subsets, and compare with the SoTA within that protocol [ 22 ]. We report standard camouflaged object detection metrics: S-measure, weighted 5F-measure, E-measure, and mean absolute error. We select checkpoints with the highest validation weighted F-measure. In line with previous works, we also benchmark final models zero-shot on a third camouflage detection dataset: NC4K [38]. Recent COD methods add task-specific priors to generic segmentation architectures, including boundary enhancement [17, 71, 22], frequency or wavelet features [53, 61], multi-scale context [45, 42 ], and foreground–background contrast or saliency disambiguation [ 22 ]. These designs reflect the need to recover subtle object boundaries and separate low-contrast camouflaged regions from visually similar backgrounds. 

Remote sensing segmentation. Unlike natural images, remote sensing imagery typically captures expansive scenes with dense, repetitive structures and objects spanning widely varying scales. Earth-Reason [ 27 ] is a large-scale geospatial pixel reasoning benchmark comprising manually annotated image masks and implicit question-answer pairs for evaluating reasoning-guided remote sensing segmentation. We use it to train, test and evaluate in this setting. In line with current SoTA in this setting [ 67 ], we report gIoU and cIoU: the average of per-sample IoUs, and the cumulative intersection over cumulative union. Prior geospatial reasoning segmentation methods use remote-sensing-specific designs such as visual token compression, multi-scale language–vision fusion, spatial-correlation-aware mask generation, and task transformations for object-, region-, and pixel-level outputs [ 27 , 63 ]. These adaptations address common properties of overhead imagery, including large spatial extent, small dense objects, and heterogeneous scene layouts. 

Challenging reasoning segmentation. Challenging reasoning segmentation requires grounding targets from complex queries involving compositional, relational, contextual, or world-knowledge reasoning. ReasonSeg-X and ReasonSeg-R [ 66 ] are recently introduced benchmarks designed to stress-test reasoning-driven segmentation beyond standard referring expressions. ReasonSeg-R refines the original ReasonSeg benchmark [ 25 ], providing test samples with corrected annotation and query issues. ReasonSeg-X extends the task with harder samples that require deeper and more systematic reasoning. Similarly to the current SoTA, we first pretrain the base model on common reasoning segmentation datasets before conducting the full range of experiments [ 66 ]. Following prior work, we report gIoU and cIoU. Recent SoTA methods for challenging reasoning segmentation invest additional computation directly into the reasoning process. In particular, StAR uses parallel test-time scaling through mask-level majority voting over multiple stochastic reasoning traces, effectively aggregating diverse candidate localizations before producing the final mask [ 66 ]. Its rollout-expanded selective tuning samples 128–256 trajectories but updates only on informative high- and low-advantage responses, mitigating weak signals when most rollouts are incorrect or uninformative. For the complete prompts across all experiments, see Appendix G. 

4.1 Experimental configurations Main experiments. For each of the three settings, we train three configurations: standard GRPO ,where we only fine-tune InternVL3.5, treating SAM3 as a frozen auxiliary tool; GRTO where we backpropagate the differentiable loss on SAM3 during GRPO as defined in Section 3; and Bootstrapped-GRTO ( B-GRTO ), where we pretrain SAM3 by pre-generating groups of rollouts with the reference policy once and backpropagating the objective in (9) on SAM3 until validation performance peaks, and proceed with standard GRTO training afterwards. In B-GRTO bootstrapping, we use experience replay [ 14 ]: rollout groups are generated once, stored, and treated as a static fine-tuning dataset. Its overhead is comparable to a single GRPO epoch, since after groups are rolled out once, the computational cost of fine-tuning SAM3 is comparatively negligible. 

Ablations. We perform two ablations to probe different training and inference regimes, conducted in the remote sensing setting, as experiments are least expensive there. The sequential training implied by B-GRTO, as derived in Section 3, should in practice produce a better aligned decoder. To verify this, we examine a different sequential training regime: first train the policy with GRPO, and after convergence, fine-tune SAM3 on deterministic policy outputs. In theory, this also produces a decoder aligned with the VLM, but not with the theoretical common objective. We call this regime Reverse-Sequential . ## 5 Discussion 

Fine-tuning the decoder is important, but it must be aligned with the policy objective. An important empirical lesson from our experiments is that the segmentation decoder should not be treated as a passive implementation detail. Even if the language model improves grounding, final masks remain limited by the decoder’s ability to handle noisy, partial, or domain-shifted prompts. Across all configurations we test, fine-tuning SAM3 yields substantial performance gains – even in the theoretically misaligned Reverse-Sequential ablation (see Section 4.1). This is also visible in Figure 1 and Appendix D: in camouflage detection, the decoder excludes fine-grained occlusions; in remote sensing, it adapts to dataset-specific annotation semantics not expressible through the conditioning mechanism; and in reasoning segmentation, it better delineates small or scattered objects. Decoder fine-tuning is most effective when it is aligned with the direction of policy optimization: reverse-Sequential also fine-tunes the decoder, but because it is not aligned with the joint objective derived in Section 3, it fails to surpass GRTO. Qualitatively, GRTO and B-GRTO improve not only the decoder but also the VLM’s tool-use behavior, leading it to produce more descriptive and concise prompts. This is clearest for B-GRTO, where a pre-aligned tool steers the VLM toward better conditioning and enables better convergence (see Appendix C). GRTO-No-Filter further supports this interpretation: removing VLM-based mask post-filtering degrades performance, suggesting that GRTO learns complementary policy–tool behaviors whose benefits depend on coordinated use. 

Bootstrapping enables cheaper and more effective optimization. We use a minimal bootstrapping strategy: populate the replay buffer once with one or two rollout epochs, then fine-tune the tool on 8this static dataset using the Section 3 objective. Even this minimal procedure yields clear gains in convergence and final performance, and the B-GRPO ablation (Section 4.1) shows that using only the bootstrapped tool during GRPO already yields a more robust system. This enables practical applications of our method even when resources are insufficient for joint training. However, B-GRPO does not replace GRTO: as the policy improves, its prompt distribution may drift from the fixed bootstrapping buffer. Joint optimization can keep adapting the decoder to this evolving distribution, leading to the best empirical results. 

B-GRTO as a general-purpose method. Across experiments, B-GRTO remains competitive with SoTA methods using domain-tailored architectural priors (Section 4). The main exception is reasoning segmentation, where our model does not reach the absolute top performance. However, this setting is sensitive to first-stage pretraining: following prior work [ 66 ], we pretrain on a small single-pass sample from a large dataset mixture (Sections 4 and E), introducing variance from the sampled subset and backbone. As shown in Table 2b, our stage-1 model starts below the reported StAR Qwen 2.5 backbone. Even so, GRTO and B-GRTO yield larger gains than StAR obtains on Qwen 2.5 with its training procedure and inference protocol, which require significantly more policy rollouts. This positions B-GRTO as a general-purpose approach for competitive cross-domain performance, combining MLLM representations with adaptable tools without domain-specific architectures or expensive test-time aggregation. 

Limitations. As with any training method, GRTO and B-GRTO run the risk of overfitting the decoder to the dataset at hand. This appears in reasoning segmentation, where the training set has only 240 samples (Section 4) and GRTO’s best checkpoint after 11 epochs (Figure 7) shows degraded zero-shot performance. This is not the case for B-GRTO, whose selected checkpoint is at epoch 6. Additionally, the bootstrapping stage depends on the quality and diversity of the initial replay buffer. If the reference policy almost never generates useful prompts for a class of examples, then BTO has limited signal for those cases. Increasing group size and sampling variance can mitigate this issue, but it increases rollout cost and storage. More adaptive replay strategies, such as refreshing the buffer after partial policy improvement or mixing reference and current-policy rollouts, may improve robustness. Finally, during hyperparameter search, we observe that the system is sensitive to mismatched learning rates between the policy and the tool. B-GRTO must therefore be applied with care, and hyperparameters should be carefully calibrated. 

Future work. In this paper, we use a deliberately simple static replay buffer, yet the resulting bootstrapped tools already improve robustness and, in several settings, lead to superior final perfor-mance. This suggests that more adaptive bootstrapping procedures may yield further gains. Potential directions are dynamic replay buffers refreshed as the policy improves, curricula for difficult or underrepresented examples, or hybrid buffers mixing reference- and intermediate-policy rollouts. Although costlier, such strategies may provide more diverse, higher-quality tool supervision, yielding a more robust decoder and stronger rewards during joint optimization. More broadly, these results indicate that investing additional computation in tool bootstrapping could be a practical way to improve final performance, especially in settings where stable tool behavior is critical for effective policy learning. 

## 6 Conclusion 

We present B-GRTO, a framework for aligning a reasoning policy with a differentiable tool. GRTO uses GRPO rollouts for both reward-based policy learning and importance-weighted tool fine-tuning, while B-GRTO cheaply bootstraps the tool to improve rewards and enhance joint optimization. Across camouflage detection, remote sensing segmentation, and challenging reasoning segmentation, fine-tuning the decoder improves over policy-only GRPO. B-GRTO is strongest overall, achieving the best final performance in two of three settings and fastest convergence in two of three settings. Further, our method establishes state-of-the-art performance in camouflage detection and remote sensing segmentation, while also providing strong gains on challenging reasoning segmentation. These results show that trainable tools should not be treated as fixed reward components in reasoning-intensive segmentation. ## A Extended Related Works 

Multimodal LLMs for Language-Instructed Segmentation. 

Early approaches to language-guided segmentation tightly couple a large language model with a pixel decoder via special segmentation tokens. LISA [ 25 ] introduce this paradigm, appending a single 

<SEG> token to the LLM’s vocabulary whose embedding is decoded by SAM [ 24 ] into a binary mask. Subsequent work extends LISA along several axes: LISA++ [ 62 ] and M2SA [ 23 ] handle multiple objects per query, and SegLLM [ 58 ] enable multi-turn conversational grounding. GLaMM [ 47 ] enrich this design with grounded captions and region-level understanding, while GSVA [ 60 ] incorporate rejection mechanisms for queries that lack a valid referent. X-SAM [ 55 ] further generalizes the architecture toward open-vocabulary and versatile segmentation. Several designs beyond a <SEG> token are explored. RSVP [ 36 ] substitutes it with a BEiT-3-encoder [56 ] over object tokens pre-detected by a MLLM. PixelLM [ 49 ] replaces it with a learned code-book of pixel embeddings for multi-target settings, and PSALM [68] adds rejection handling to it. A common limitation across this entire family is that all components are optimized with standard cross-entropy on fixed annotation sets, which can overfit to the label distribution, limiting generalization, and does not allow the model to explore novel approaches that could lead to superior results. 

Decoder as a tool and Reinforcement Learning. 

SAM4LLM [ 5 ] is one of the first to train an MLLM to output bounding-boxes to prompt a frozen SAM2 [ 48 ], using it as an external tool. While they used supervised fine-tuning (SFT), the success of group relative policy optimization (GRPO) [ 52 ] for reasoning in language models has inspired a second generation of segmentation approaches that use it to learn robust and accurate tool-use of SAM. Seg-Zero [ 31 ] pioneer this regime, training the reasoning VLM with bounding-box IoU rewards, and using SAM itself only at inference. VisionReasoner [ 32 ] and POPEN [ 72 ] extend Seg-Zero’s multi-object handling, with POPEN using DPO [ 46 ] on top of a LISA-pretrained backbone. SAM-R1 [ 21 ]and StAR [ 66 ] extend the approach to end-to-end rewards, evaluating SAM during training and using its mask IoU as reward. LENS [ 73 ] and CoRPS [ 37 ] suggest changing the prompting approach by adding a learnable context encoder and a correlation prior, respectively. To train introduced modules, they backpropagate a segmentation loss while tuning a MLLM with GRPO under mask-IoU rewards. Several attempts are made to employ these methods for specialized domains, e.g. RemoteReasoner [ 63 ] and Think2Seg [ 67 ] in the remote sensing domain, or PathChat-SegR1[ 35 ] in the pathology domain. SAM3 agent [ 2] suggests interacting with the segmentation decoder in a multi-turn, agentic manner. SAM-Veteran [ 10 ] improves these interactions by multi-turn GRPO training, rewarding both mask and box quality across dialogue turns. RSAgent [ 18 ] improves it further by adding agentic perception. 14 SegAgent [ 74 ] explores a complementary agent formulation using Tree-of-Thoughts planning and a hybrid RL/SFT objective, enabling iterative mask refinement without gradient flow through SAM. These approaches demonstrate that RL-shaped language reasoning measurably lifts segmentation quality even when the segmentation tool itself is held fixed. However, most of the methods practically assume SAM to be a perfect tool and do not explore the potential gain in treating SAM as a trainable component. 

SAM Fine-Tuning for Specialized Domains. 

Although SAM exhibits strong zero-shot segmentation capabilities, its performance degrades on out-of-distribution imagery. Fine-tuning SAM on well-camouflaged animals, cast shadows, and medical images is shown to substantially recover accuracy in these failure modes [ 3]. Analogously, in remote sensing, fine-tuned SAM variants outperform prompt-tuned baselines on building footprints, solar panels, sinkholes, and dead-tree detection [ 1]. However, these studies assume perfect prompts, whereas in real-world scenarios prompts are often ambiguous or too long to be tokenized by SAM. These findings motivate our joint fine-tuning of SAM 3 alongside the language backbone. 

Joint RL for dense outputs beyond segmentation. 

The composition of vision-language understanding and dense generation has been studied beyond segmentation. FireScope [ 41 ] attaches a U-Net [ 50 ] decoder to a reasoning model to generate wildfire risk maps, the MLLM and decoder are trained in stages with GRPO and regression loss respectively. Janus [ 59 , 4] interleave visual understanding and image generation within a single autoregressive transformer with a joint vocabulary. Several works [ 11 , 44 , 39 ] have applied GRPO to the Janus architecture for joint reasoning and image generation. Bagel [ 8] similarly unite language tokens with normalization flows trained by flow matching for language-instructed image-to-image tasks. Several approaches [ 30 , 64 ] apply RL to Bagel with summed objectives of text GRPO and Flow-GRPO [ 29 ]. While these methods target generation rather than segmentation, they support the general finding that joint fine-tuning improves the quality of language-instructed dense outputs.

## E Detailed experimental setup 

Across all experiments, we fine-tune SAM3’s semantic mask decoder, keeping the rest of the model frozen. We use intersection over union (IoU) as our GRPO reward with only a small validity 17 Figure 7: Evaluation performance for reasoning segmentation trainings. Tracked metric is mean between gIoU and cIoU. Best checkpoint is marked with a star. For bootstrapped runs the evaluations of the GRTO stage are reported. The cost of bootstrapping is negligible compared to joint training. component to encourage valid output formats, without supervising intermediate conditioning to allow the VLM to freely discover the most optimal conditioning strategy. To train the tool with a differentiable reward surrogate, we combine soft-IoU with pixel-wise binary cross-entropy (BCE) between the predicted foreground mask and the ground-truth foreground mask. Detailed descriptions of reward and loss are available below. Both primarily encourage overlap between the predicted and target masks. We conduct each experiment on eight NVIDIA H200 GPUs for up to 30 hours. Across all experiments, we set the KL-divergence weight β = 0 .01 , and we use a validation set to evaluate on every epoch. We train until validation performance peaks, and benchmark the best validation checkpoint. 

Remote sensing and camouflage detection. In the remote sensing and camouflage detection settings, We use a global batch size of 64 unique prompts per batch and a group size of 8, amounting to a total of 512 generations per batch. We set the maximum output length to 1024 tokens. For the bootstrapping stage, our experience replay consists of 8 generations per group. In the remote sensing setting, where the training set has six unique prompts for every image, one epoch consists of one randomly sampled prompt per image for robustness during training. We use a learning rate of 

1 × 10 −6 for InternVL3.5 across all trainings, and a learning rate of 1 × 10 −4 for SAM3 for all trainings except second-stage B-GRTO for remote sensing where we set SAM3’s learning rate to 

1 × 10 −6, because the dynamic epochs already yield a nearly converged model. We train for 12 epoch on remote sensing and 9 epochs on camouflage. We perform full-model training on the VLM. For camouflage detection, we separate 100 samples from COD10K and 35 samples from CAMO as validation set, mixing the train and validation splits of the two datasets during training. This results in 3825 training and 135 validation samples. The testing samples are of size 1974 and 250, with 4121 additional zero-shot samples from NC4K. EarthReason consists of 2,731, 1135, and 1928 training, testing and validation images with ~6 prompts per image. We take the full training and testing sets, and a sample of 500 unique images and prompts from the validation set to balance validation robustness with speed. We select checkpoints with the highest mean validation performance across gIoU and cIoU. 

Challenging reasoning segmentation. In the challenging reasoning segmentation setting, in line with the state-of-the-art [ 66 ], we run a single-epoch first-stage VLM-only GRPO on InternVL3.5-8 on 10K samples from RefCocog [ 40 ], gRefCoco [ 28 ], and LVIS [ 16 ] without KL divergence and with a learning rate of 1 × 10 −5. We use a global batch size of 16 unique prompts and a group size of 16 for a total of 256 generations per batch, and set the maximum output length to 2048. For the bootstrapping stage, our experience replay consists of 32 generation per group – double the amount compared to other trainings to minimize the number of groups with all-zero IoU. For our experiments after pretraining, we set the learning rates of both SAM3 and InternVL3.5-8B to 5 × 10 −5, except for the second stage of B-GRTO, where SAM3’s learning rate is set to 1 × 10 −6.
