# Recursive Block-Diagonal Coupling for Resource-Efficient Training of Vision Models 

Maxim Henry , Adrien Deliège , Sébastien Piérard , and Marc Van Droogenbroeck 

Montefiore Institute, University of Liège, Liège, Belgium {Maxim.Henry, Adrien.Deliege, S.Pierard, M.VanDroogenbroeck}@uliege.be 

Abstract. Training high-capacity vision models from scratch requires substantial computational resources. To improve training efficiency of a wide target model, existing growth methods often assume the avail-ability of narrower models, obscuring the true computational cost of the entire pipeline. We propose an efficient training protocol, RBDC, that builds wide models by coupling in a parameter-free block-diagonal way narrower, independently trained models in a recursive way. This allows a flexible allocation of the training budget available across all the models involved. Evaluated with vision transformers (DeiT) and convolutional networks (ResNet) on ImageNet, our RBDC training protocol shows a much better efficiency than models trained from scratch with the stan-dard protocol, yielding 30% FLOPs reduction at similar test accuracies. It also achieves higher performances at same training FLOPs than train-ing protocols from the model growth literature. Finally, we show that our models can serve as better backbones than their original counterparts for downstream object detection and instance segmentation tasks. 

Keywords: Efficient Training · Recursive Training · FLOPs · Trans-former · CNN · Model Weights Initialization 

## 1 Introduction 

Deep learning has achieved remarkable success across computer vision tasks, largely driven by the upscaling of models. However, model upscaling requires a proportional increase in computing power. Hardware limits are approached, and the financial cost of training high-capacity models from scratch and using them grows [27]. It is thus essential to improve the efficiency of such models, and in our case, those used in computer vision ( e.g ., transformers, CNNs). According to the Sustainability Directory [25], “resource-efficient machine learning, at its core, is about optimizing machine learning processes to minimize resource consumption without sacrificing performance”. However, a distinction must be made between the training efficiency and the inference efficiency. In this work, we aim at improving resource efficiency during training in the sense of

Specifically, we measure performance via test accuracy and resources via floating-point operations (FLOPs). Training efficiency depends on several elements that govern the overall train-ing protocol ( e.g ., number of epochs, model architecture, dataset size, etc.). Various training protocols can be defined. For example, the standard protocol 

to train a vision model from scratch consists of initializing randomly its weights and then training them. We then call target model the final, trained model ready for deployment. The standard training protocol is not the only way to obtain this target model. Wide-to-narrow approaches, such as pruning, initially train a wide model (characterized by a much larger parameter count than the target model) and prune it into the narrower target model (having fewer parameters). narrow model that is progressively expanded into the wider target model. In both cases, the target model remains the final objective, while the terms narrow

Our work is closer to the latter case, and, contrary to training protocols based on fine-tuning pre-trained models, such as parameter-efficient fine-tuning [14, 15, 33], we focus on training models from scratch .

Contributions. Our goal is to develop a training protocol that improves the trade-off between the number of FLOPs used for training and the test accuracy reached by the trained model. In particular, our contributions are threefold. First, we introduce Recursive Block-Diagonal Coupling (RBDC) as an effi-cient training protocol. RBDC constructs and trains high-capacity models recur-sively by independently training distinct narrow models and coupling them into a wide model using a block-diagonal initialization with zero-padded interaction terms, as illustrated in Fig. 1. This parameter-free initialization allows to start the optimization of the wide model from an “educated” state, rather than from a random initialization as done in the standard protocol. Second, we show that our RBDC training protocol is much more efficient than the standard one, as also depicted in Fig. 1, reducing by up to 30% the training FLOPs needed to reach a high test accuracy on ImageNet with major computer vision model architectures, i.e . transformers (DeiT) and CNNs (ResNet). Third, we show that the benefits of our RBDC training protocol extend beyond improving training efficiency. Specifically, models trained with RBDC can serve as well-performing pretrained backbones for downstream tasks such as object detection and instance segmentation on the COCO benchmark [17], with performances on par or better than models trained with the standard protocol. 

## 2 Related Work 

2.1 Inference Efficiency vs. Training Efficiency Inference Efficiency. A significant portion of research focuses on the efficiency of a model after deployment (inference) [20,26], with the objective of maximizing performance within a fixed computational inference budget. Common inference-efficient approaches include structured pruning [10, 12] and knowledge distilla-tion [8] to reduce model size for deployment. However, such top-down paradigms require an initial (very) wide model or teacher, whose training consumes a high amount of resources. Similarly, post-training quantization [1] reduces the pre-cision of model weights to lower memory footprint and latency but remains an inference-centric optimization. While effective for deployment, these techniques generally do not alleviate the computational burden of the initial training phase. 

Training Efficiency. A parallel stream of research, including ours, focuses on methods to improve training efficiency [9, 24]. This focus is important because relying on model scaling for performance gains requires an exponential increase in computing power [28]. Furthermore, recent economic models for computer 4 M. Henry et al. 

vision systems demonstrate that the financial cost of training wide models from scratch can create significant barriers to deployment [27]. This computational scaling is evident in standard architectures: progressing from a vision transformer base (ViT-B, 86M parameters, 17.5 GFLOPs) to a large (307M parameters, 61.5 GFLOPs) or huge (632M parameters, 167 GFLOPs) increases training resource consumption [30]. Efforts to mitigate these costs include parameter-centric and model-centric approaches, as described hereafter. 

2.2 Parameter-Centric Training Efficiency Low-Precision Training. Distinct from quantization for inference, low-pre-cision training aims to accelerate gradient calculation during the optimization process. Standard techniques now leverage mixed-precision (FP16/BF16) [21]. More aggressive approaches, such as FP8 training [22], exploit modern hard-ware capabilities to further reduce memory footprint, though they often require specialized stabilization techniques to prevent numerical underflow during back-propagation. Though effective, low-precision training is hardware-dependent and aims to reduce the cost per FLOP, while we focus on reducing the amount of FLOPs. Both can be naturally paired, meaning our RBDC training protocol with narrow models and the wide target model could in principle be trained in mixed-precision for compounded acceleration. 

Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) [33]. Rather than updating all weights during training steps, PEFT methods target a small subset of parame-ters. Techniques like Adapters [14] and LoRA [15] freeze the pre-trained back-bone and inject small, trainable modules. While these methods reduce the pa-rameters updated per step, they are traditionally reserved for fine-tuning rather than for training from scratch. The block-diagonal coupling of our RBDC train-ing protocol draws conceptual inspiration from the zero-initialization of interac-tion terms in LoRA but applies it much more extensively in a recursive way. 

2.3 Model-Centric Training Efficiency Progressive Growing. Training wide models from scratch prevents leverag-ing narrower models trained with fewer resources. Hence, recent research ex-plores initializing wide architectures using weights from pre-trained narrower ones. A common approach is function-preserving growth . Techniques such as Net2Net [3] and progressive stacking [7] expand the width or depth of a single model while maintaining its exact function at the moment of expansion. For instance, Net2Net achieves this by duplicating existing units and breaking sym-metry with small noise perturbations. Other methods, such as GradMax [5], ini-tialize new capacity by setting incoming weights to zero to preserve the model’s function, while using gradient information to mathematically optimize the out-going weights. This allows the wider model to bypass the initial chaotic phase of convergence. However, these methods typically focus on expanding a single model instance. Consequently, the expanded model is constrained by the singular Resource-Efficient Training of Vision Models 5

feature space learned by its precursor. In contrast, our RBDC training protocol benefits from the learned knowledge of multiple narrower models. 

Recombining Learned Parameters. A more recent paradigm involves syn-thesizing a wider model by aggregating multiple narrower, pre-trained models. Unlike standard ensembling, which averages outputs, these methods integrate pa-rameters into a single unified architecture. Approaches like MixtureGrowth [23] demonstrate that recombining distinct narrower models can act as a powerful initialization for a wider student. However, such methods typically assume that at least one of the narrow models is already available in an existing model zoo, using this assumption to justify the training speedup. Accounting for the re-sources needed to train the narrow models is a saner practice and reveals more moderate benefits. In that setting, we show that our RBDC training protocol provides an efficiency gain even when accounting for the total computational budget required to train all narrow models completely from scratch. 

## 3 Method 

In line with the model-centric approaches for training efficiency, we propose a recursive training protocol, RBDC, that constructs high-capacity target mod-els by combining narrower, independently trained narrow models. Unlike prior recombination protocols that assume the availability of pre-trained models in a zoo [3, 23], our framework is designed to yield computational efficiency when training entirely from scratch. Essentially, RBDC leverages the observation that training multiple narrower models is computationally cheaper than training a single wide model from scratch. By training these narrow models with distinct initializations, we capture a diverse set of features. By initializing the wider tar-get model via a block-diagonal formulation to benefit from the independently learned features of the narrow models, we observe that we can afford to effectively reduce the total FLOPs required to train the model at no performance drop, as we will show in Sec. 4. The pseudocode of RBDC, which complements Fig. 1, is given in Algorithm 1 and is further explained hereafter. 

3.1 Recursive Width Halving 

RBDC training protocol operates on the principle of recursive width halving. Let us consider a target model with width W (e.g ., channel count in CNNs or embedding dimension in transformers). Instead of initializing it randomly to train it, we initialize it with two narrower models, each with width W/ 2.These narrow models are themselves initialized with narrower models, which are also initialized with even narrower models, recursively. When the narrowest initializing models are small enough, or cannot be halved anymore, they are randomly initialized with distinct seeds and trained independently following the standard protocol. After training, they are distinct experts capturing potentially different features, and they are then coupled to serve as an educated initialization for training the wider model from which they originate. 6 M. The use of zero initialization for off-diagonal blocks follows other works, no-tably Net2Net [3], which aims to grow a model by ensuring that identity is preserved before training the grown model. In our case, it indeed ensures that at the start of the wide model training phase, the model behaves as an ensemble of the two narrow models. The forward pass of the wide model is almost equivalent to the independent execution of the narrow models, up to minor differences in normalization layers’ statistics. The zero initialization also echoes parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods like LoRA [15], which initialize trainable adapters to zero to preserve the pre-trained model’s function. In our case, although the en-tire combined matrix remains fully trainable, this initialization allows the wide model to gradually learn the correlations between the features of the narrow models without degrading the initial performance inherited from them. Finally, this design choice was also guided by our experiments, and we further show its advantage over a random initialization in Sec. 4.

## 4 Results 

Target Models. We evaluate RBDC on both vision transformers (DeiT) and CNNs (ResNet). Throughout this section, we use tiny , small , base , and large to refer to the different sizes of DeiT [29, 30] and 0.125x, 0.25x, 0.5x, and 1.0x to refer to the different sizes of ResNet-50D [34] where the multiplicator used applies to the width of each convolutional layer, with 1.0x being the target model. 

Training Efficiency through Normalized FLOPs. To ensure a rigorous and fair comparison between training protocols, we account for the computational cost of training every model involved, including all narrow models. We use the 8 M. Henry et al. 

fvcore library to compute the forward pass cost in multiply-accumulate opera-tions (MACs). Because fvcore reports MACs under the FLOPs nomenclature, we adhere to the definition where 1 MAC represents exactly 2 FLOPs. These values represent the cost of a single forward pass. To estimate the total training compute, we apply the standard approximation established in the literature [13]: the backward pass requires approximately twice the compute of the forward pass to calculate gradients regarding both activations and weights. Therefore, the to-tal compute per training step (one forward and one backward) is three times the forward cost. The total FLOPs for a complete training phase are thus calculated as 3 ×forward FLOPs ×number of epochs ×training images. Throughout this sec-tion, we report these costs as normalized FLOPs as done in [23]. Specifically, we calculate the training FLOPs for a given experiment and divide it by the total FLOPs required to train the baseline target model from scratch (trained for 300 epochs for DeiT and 90 epochs for ResNet) with the standard training protocol, providing a clear relative measure of training efficiency. 

Experiments. We present our results across 4 experiments: (1) we show the training efficiency of our RBDC training protocol in Sec. 4.1; (2) we analyze the impact of our off-diagonal blocks initialization and training ratio r in Sec. 4.2; (3) we compare RBDC against training protocols from the model growth litera-ture in Sec. 4.3; (4) we validate the usefulness of the models trained with RBDC on downstream tasks in Sec. 4.4. 

4.1 Resource Efficiency of RBDC 

The objective of our 1 st experiment is to show that, regarding the standard protocol, RBDC can train a model with a similar accuracy while requiring a much smaller amount of FLOPs. 

Experimental Setup. We use the DeiT models [29] that we train on Image-Net-1K with the official DeiT codebase and their optimal setup [6]. Optimization is performed using AdamW [19] with a base learning rate of 5e-4 and a weight decay of 0.05 and an effective batch size of 1024. The learning rate follows a cosine annealing schedule [18], preceded by a 5-epoch linear warmup. For RBDC, during the transition phase where narrow models are coupled to initialize a wide model, the training process is treated as an entirely new run. The optimizer state is not carried over, and the learning rate schedule—including the 5-epoch linear warmup and subsequent cosine decay—is restarted from scratch. We consider DeiT-Base as the target model. With our RBDC training pro-tocol and one recursive step, we first train DeiT-Small models and couple them to obtain a DeiT-Base model. With two recursive steps, RBDC couples DeiT-Tiny models to obtain the DeiT-Small models, further coupled into a DeiT-Base model. This whole training is therefore denoted Tiny →Small →Base. We com-pare our results with a baseline that corresponds to the training of a DeiT-Base model with the standard protocol. 4.3 Comparison with Model Growth Training Protocols 

The objective of our 3 rd experiment is to benchmark our method against the closest related literature: the model growth literature [3, 5, 23, 32]. Our target is a ResNet-50D model, and the training is performed on ImageNet-1K. 

Implementation Details. Following the protocol in [23], we grow from a half-width ResNet-50D (0.5 ×) to a full-width ResNet-50D (1.0 ×) when using a single recursive step. For more recursive steps, we recursively apply this process, halving the initial width at each additional step ( e.g ., a 2-step process shrinks from 1.0 × to 0.5 × and then to 0.25 ×, and a 3-step, finally, to 0.125 ×), while consistently maintaining the training ratio r = 2 . Unlike MixtureGrowth, which relies on template mixing and linear combinations to generate new weights, our method uses a parameter-free block-diagonal zero-initialization.

We integrate the pre-trained DeiT-Base backbone utilizing the ViT-Det approach [16] to adapt the plain Vision Transformer ( i.e ., the pre-trained DeiT-Base backbone) for dense prediction tasks. Specifically, we apply windowed attention (with a window size of 14) in blocks 0, 1, 3, 4, 6, 7, 9, and 10, while leaving the remaining blocks as global attention to propagate information across the image. A SimpleFPN neck [16] is used to build a multi-scale feature pyra-mid from the single-scale 768-dimensional output of the backbone. Although our narrow and wide models, alongside the baseline, were pre-trained using the DeiT distillation objective (introducing an additional distillation token), the ViTDet architecture does not utilize the resulting distillation token. Training Configuration. The model is trained for 36 epochs using standard resizing (short edge of 800 pixels, maximum long edge of 1,333 pixels), following the baseline training recipes established for Mask R-CNN [11]. Optimization is performed using AdamW [19] with a base learning rate of 1e-4, a weight decay of 0.1 and an effective batch size of 16. We apply a cosine annealing learning rate schedule [19] that decays smoothly to 0.0, preceded by a linear warmup over the first 500 iterations. To preserve the spatial features learned during pre-training, we employ layer-wise learning rate decay [2, 4] with a decay rate of 0.7 across the 12 transformer blocks of the backbone. ## 5 Conclusion 

We introduced RBDC, an efficient training protocol to train high-capacity com-puter vision models from scratch. By leveraging independently trained narrow models and coupling them in a block-diagonal way to serve as educated initial-ization for training wider models, RBDC constructs target models recursively. That initialization is parameter-free, as it uses zero-padded interaction terms to preserve the learned features of the narrow models. RBDC enables to use train-ing ratios between wide and narrow models that efficiently control the amount of FLOPs needed to train the target model. Our results show that when fully accounting for the complete FLOPs budget of all training phases, our RBDC training protocol improves the trade-off curve over standard training, yielding up to a 30% FLOPs reduction at comparable test accuracies, and even a moderate performance improvement at similar FLOPs budgets. On ImageNet, we achieve competitive performance with both DeiT and ResNet models without relying on pre-trained models or parameter-heavy tech-niques, outperforming training protocols of the model growth literature in that regard. Furthermore, our trained models transfer successfully to downstream tasks when used as backbones, yielding improved object detection and instance segmentation performances on COCO [17] over the standard baselines. Finally, we underline that our RBDC training protocol uses mechanisms that could be generalized beyond computer vision models. In particular, model ar-chitectures using mostly linear operations, such as transformers used by large Resource-Efficient Training of Vision Models 15 

language models, could also benefit from RBDC. Given the cost of training such models, a training FLOPs reduction of 30% could yield spectacular savings.
