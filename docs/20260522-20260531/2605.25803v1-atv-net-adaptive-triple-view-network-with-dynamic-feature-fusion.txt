A B S T R A C T 

Recent semantic segmentation research has increasingly moved toward stronger context mod-eling, dense attention, and transformer-based architectures. Although these models achieve impressive performance, classical CNN-based segmentation pipelines remain attractive because of their simplicity, efficiency, and ease of implementation. This paper revisits a practical question: how far can a ResNet-based segmentation model be improved by only modifying the segmentation head? We propose ATV-Net, an Adaptive Triple-View Network that strengthens a ResNet-101 backbone using three simple but complementary receptive-field views. The micro view captures point-wise semantic responses, the local view models neighborhood structures and object boundaries, and the scout view provides enlarged contextual cues. Instead of fusing these views with fixed weights, ATV-Net introduces an Adaptive Decision Gate that dynamically selects receptive-field responses according to input scene characteristics. A compact global coordination layer is further applied to improve spatial and semantic consistency. Experiments on the Cityscapes validation set show that ATV-Net achieves 80.31% mIoU. This result suggests that classical CNN-based segmentation is still far from obsolete: with simple receptive-field views and adaptive fusion, a ResNet-based pipeline can reach a competitive accuracy level without relying on transformer-style global attention or overly complex context modules. 

## 1. Introduction 

Semantic segmentation is a fundamental task in computer vision. Its goal is to assign a semantic category to every pixel in an image, enabling detailed scene understanding for autonomous driving, intelligent transportation, robotics, and urban perception. In street-view scenarios, segmentation models must recognize both large regions such as roads, buildings, vegetation, and sky, and small or thin objects such as poles, traffic signs, pedestrians, riders, and traffic lights. This makes high-resolution urban scene parsing especially challenging. Early CNN-based segmentation methods established the foundation of modern dense prediction. Fully convolutional networks converted image classification backbones into pixel-level predictors [1], while later encoder-decoder and dilated-convolution methods improved spatial resolution and contextual representation [8, 3, 4]. Classical context aggregation methods such as PSPNet [2] and DeepLab [3] further demonstrated that multi-scale context is critical for semantic segmentation. PSPNet introduced pyramid pooling to aggregate contextual information from different spatial regions, while DeepLabv3 used atrous spatial pyramid pooling to capture multi-scale context through parallel atrous convolutions. These methods significantly improved CNN-based segmentation performance and became important baselines for later research. However, many classical context modules use a fixed aggregation strategy. Once the architecture is designed, different receptive-field branches are usually combined in a static way. This may be suboptimal because different urban scenes require different types of visual evidence. A boundary-heavy image may benefit more from local structural cues, while a scene with distant or occluded objects may require broader contextual information. A single fixed fusion rule cannot always adapt to these variations. More recently, transformer-based segmentation models have achieved strong results by introducing global attention and more flexible feature interaction [6, 7]. For example, SegFormer combines a hierarchical transformer encoder with a lightweight decoder and shows strong performance on standard segmentation benchmarks [6]. These models demonstrate the power of global representation learning. Nevertheless, transformer-style architectures may also introduce additional complexity, stronger pretraining dependence, or higher

In this paper, we revisit this question from the perspective of receptive-field design. We observe that many useful segmentation cues can be captured by simple receptive-field patterns. A point-wise projection is suitable for semantic transformation, a local convolution is effective for boundary and neighborhood modeling, and a moderately enlarged receptive field can provide useful contextual cues. However, the key is not to use these receptive fields independently or fuse them statically. Instead, they should be organized as complementary views and selected adaptively according to the input scene. Based on this idea, we propose ATV-Net, an Adaptive Triple-View Network. ATV-Net is built on a ResNet-101 backbone [13] and introduces a simple adaptive segmentation head. The head contains three branches: a micro view, a local view, and a scout view. These branches capture point-wise semantics, local structures, and enlarged context, respectively. An Adaptive Decision Gate then generates input-dependent fusion weights for the three views. By doing so, ATV-Net strengthens a classical ResNet-based segmentation pipeline while avoiding heavy attention blocks or complex context aggregation modules. The main message of this work is not that simple receptive fields alone are sufficient. Instead, ATV-Net shows that simple receptive fields can become highly effective when they are complementary and adaptively fused. With this design, ATV-Net achieves 80.31% mIoU on the Cityscapes validation set [5], indicating that CNN-based segmentation pipelines can still produce strong results with careful and adaptive head design. The contributions of this paper are summarized as follows: 

• We revisit classical CNN-based semantic segmentation and show that a ResNet-based pipeline can still be substantially strengthened with a simple adaptive segmentation head. 

• We propose a Triple-View Head that uses micro, local, and scout views to capture point-wise semantic cues, local geometric structures, and enlarged contextual information. 

• We introduce an Adaptive Decision Gate that dynamically fuses different receptive-field views according to input-dependent scene characteristics. 

• We report a Cityscapes validation result of 80.31% mIoU, suggesting that adaptive receptive-field fusion is an effective design for CNN-based semantic segmentation.. 

## 2. Related Work 

2.1. CNN-Based Semantic Segmentation 

CNN-based semantic segmentation has developed from fully convolutional prediction to increasingly stronger dense prediction frameworks. Fully convolutional networks first showed that classification networks could be trans-formed into pixel-level predictors by replacing fully connected layers with convolutional operations [1]. Later segmen-tation models improved this formulation by using stronger backbones, encoder-decoder structures, skip connections, and dilated convolutions [8, 4]. These methods established CNNs as a powerful foundation for semantic segmentation. A key challenge in CNN-based segmentation is the trade-off between semantic abstraction and spatial precision. Deep layers provide strong semantic representations but reduce spatial resolution, while shallow layers preserve details but contain weaker semantic information. To address this issue, many methods preserve high-resolution features using dilated convolution or recover details using decoder structures. ATV-Net follows this CNN-based direction and uses a ResNet-101 backbone with dilated convolution to maintain dense feature maps for high-resolution prediction. 

2.2. Multi-Scale Context Aggregation 

Multi-scale context aggregation is important because objects and scene structures appear at different scales. PSPNet introduced pyramid pooling to gather context from multiple spatial regions [2]. DeepLabv3 introduced atrous spatial pyramid pooling, which uses multiple atrous convolution rates to capture features with different effective receptive fields [3]. These designs showed that context modeling is essential for improving segmentation accuracy. ATV-Net is related to these multi-scale designs but differs in two aspects. First, ATV-Net uses a simpler triple-view formulation rather than a heavy pyramid or dense multi-rate module. Second, ATV-Net does not treat all receptive-field responses as fixed contributions. Instead, the Adaptive Decision Gate learns to adjust the importance of each view based on the input feature distribution. In this sense, ATV-Net can be viewed as a simple adaptive alternative to fixed multi-scale context aggregation.

2.3. Transformer-Based Segmentation 

Transformer-based segmentation methods have recently achieved impressive performance by introducing self-attention, hierarchical attention, and global feature interaction [6, 16, 7]. These models are effective because attention can model long-range dependencies and capture flexible contextual relationships. SegFormer, for example, combines a hierarchical transformer encoder with a lightweight MLP decoder, showing that transformer features can be powerful for dense prediction [6]. However, this paper does not aim to compete directly with large transformer-based segmentation models. Instead, ATV-Net focuses on a different question: whether a classical ResNet-based segmentation pipeline can still be improved through a simple and adaptive head design. This positioning is important because CNN-based models remain useful when simplicity, efficiency, and implementation accessibility are desired. 

2.4. Dynamic Feature Fusion 

Dynamic feature fusion allows a network to adjust feature weighting according to the input. Related ideas have been explored in channel recalibration and selective receptive-field modeling, where feature responses or convolutional branches are adaptively weighted according to input content [17, 18]. In semantic segmentation, dynamic fusion can help a model emphasize different features for different images or regions. ATV-Net adopts a compact form of dynamic feature fusion. Rather than performing dense self-attention, ATV-Net uses a view-level decision gate. The gate summarizes the input feature map and produces three weights corresponding to the micro, local, and scout views. This design keeps the fusion mechanism simple while allowing the model to adaptively select useful receptive-field responses. 

## 3. Method 

3.1. Overview 

The overall architecture of ATV-Net is illustrated in Fig. 1. ATV-Net is designed as a simple adaptive semantic segmentation framework built upon a classical CNN backbone. Given an input image, the network first extracts dense feature representations using a high-resolution ResNet-101 backbone. The extracted feature map is then processed by a Triple-View Head, which generates three complementary receptive-field views for semantic reasoning. Specifically, the micro view focuses on point-wise semantic transformation, the local view captures neighborhood structures and boundary information, and the scout view provides enlarged contextual cues through a larger receptive field. These three views are dynamically fused by the proposed Adaptive Decision Gate, which generates input-dependent fusion weights according to scene characteristics and feature statistics. After adaptive fusion, a compact Global Coordination module further refines the fused representation and improves spatial consistency before the final segmentation prediction.

3.6. Training Objective 

ATV-Net is trained using pixel-wise cross-entropy loss with Online Hard Example Mining (OHEM) [14]. OHEM selects difficult pixels with high prediction loss and focuses optimization on these regions. This is useful for urban scene segmentation because easy background regions often dominate the training objective, while small objects and ambiguous boundaries are more important for improving accuracy. ## 4. Experiments 

4.1. Dataset 

We evaluate ATV-Net on the Cityscapes dataset, a standard benchmark for semantic urban scene understanding [5]. Cityscapes contains high-resolution street-view images collected from multiple cities and provides fine pixel-level annotations for semantic segmentation. The dataset includes diverse urban objects such as roads, sidewalks, buildings, vehicles, pedestrians, riders, poles, traffic signs, vegetation, and sky. 

4.2. Evaluation Metric 

We use mean Intersection over Union (mIoU) as the evaluation metric. For each class, IoU is computed as: 

𝐼𝑜𝑈 = 𝑇 𝑃 𝑇 𝑃 + 𝐹 𝑃 + 𝐹 𝑁 , (14) where 𝑇 𝑃 , 𝐹 𝑃 , and 𝐹 𝑁 denote true positives, false positives, and false negatives. The final mIoU is obtained by averaging IoU over all semantic classes. 

4.3. Implementation Details 

ATV-Net is implemented with a ResNet-101 backbone [13] and uses an output stride of 8 to preserve dense spatial features for high-resolution segmentation. The input images are randomly cropped to 768 × 768 during training. Standard data augmentation is applied, including random scaling, random cropping, and horizontal flipping. All experiments are performed on a single NVIDIA RTX 5080 GPU. The model is trained with a batch size of 8 for 150 epochs. We use AdamW [12] as the optimizer, with an initial learning rate of 1 × 10 −4 . A poly learning rate schedule is adopted throughout training. For the segmentation head, ATV-Net uses three receptive-field branches, including a 1 × 1 micro view, a 3 × 3 local view, and a dilated 5 × 5 scout view. The Adaptive Decision Gate dynamically generates view-level fusion weights using global feature statistics and softmax normalization. The network is trained with OHEM-based cross-entropy loss to emphasize hard pixels and boundary-sensitive regions.

4.4. Main Result 

Table 1 reports the Cityscapes validation result of ATV-Net. ATV-Net achieves 80.31% mIoU on the Cityscapes validation set. This result supports the central idea of this paper: a classical ResNet-based segmentation model can still reach a strong accuracy level when equipped with a simple adaptive receptive-field fusion head. This result should not be interpreted as a direct claim of superiority over all modern segmentation models. Transformer-based methods and heavily optimized segmentation frameworks may achieve higher accuracy. Instead, the value of ATV-Net lies in its simplicity. With only a simple modification after the backbone, ATV-Net demonstrates that adaptive fusion of basic receptive-field views can effectively improve CNN-based segmentation.

4.5. Discussion 

ATV-Net provides a different perspective from increasingly complex segmentation architectures. Rather than increas-ing model complexity through dense attention or heavy context modules, ATV-Net focuses on organizing simple receptive fields more effectively. The micro, local, and scout views correspond to three useful levels of visual evidence: point-wise semantics, local geometry, and enlarged context. The Adaptive Decision Gate makes the fusion process input-dependent, allowing the model to adjust its behavior across different scenes. Compared with fixed multi-scale aggregation modules, ATV-Net emphasizes adaptive selection, which provides additional flexibility for handling different scene structures and semantic ambiguities. Some scenes contain many boundary-sensitive regions, while others require broader contextual reasoning for accurate prediction. By dynamically adjusting view-level feature weighting, ATV-Net provides a simple yet effective alternative to fixed receptive-field fusion strategies. Further analysis can include more detailed studies on different fusion strategies, view configurations, and adaptive weighting mechanisms. 

## 5. Conclusion 

In this paper, we presented ATV-Net, an Adaptive Triple-View Network that strengthens classical CNN-based semantic segmentation through simple receptive-field views and dynamic decision gating. ATV-Net revisits the ResNet-based segmentation pipeline and shows that strong performance can still be achieved without relying on transformer-style global attention or overly complex context modules. By combining micro, local, and scout views, ATV-Net captures point-wise semantics, local structures, and enlarged contextual cues. The Adaptive Decision Gate dynamically fuses these views according to input scene characteristics. Experiments on the Cityscapes validation set show that ATV-Net achieves 80.31% mIoU, suggesting that adaptive receptive-field fusion is a promising and efficient direction for CNN-based semantic segmentation.
